Definition: Group

A group is an algebraic structure ⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩\alg{G; 1, {}^{-1}, \cdot}⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩ consisting of:

  • a set GGG, called the underlying set;
  • a distinguished element 1∈G1 \in G1∈G, called the identity element;
  • a unary operation −1:G→G{}^{-1}: G \to G−1:G→G, written as x↦x−1x \mapsto x^{-1}x↦x−1, called inversion;
  • a binary operation ⋅:G×G→G\cdot : G \times G \to G⋅:G×G→G, written as (x,y)↦x⋅y(x, y) \mapsto x \cdot y(x,y)↦x⋅y, called the group operation or group product;

satisfying the following requirements:

  • Associative property: (x⋅y)⋅z=x⋅(y⋅z)(x \cdot y) \cdot z = x \cdot (y \cdot z)(x⋅y)⋅z=x⋅(y⋅z) for all x,y,z∈Gx, y, z \in Gx,y,z∈G.
  • Identity property: 1⋅x=x⋅1=x1 \cdot x = x \cdot 1 = x1⋅x=x⋅1=x for all x∈Gx \in Gx∈G.
  • Inverse property: x⋅x−1=x−1⋅x=1x \cdot x^{-1} = x^{-1} \cdot x = 1x⋅x−1=x−1⋅x=1 for all x∈Gx \in Gx∈G.

Definition: Rng

A rng (pronounced as “rung”) is an algebraic structure ⟨R;0,−,+,⋅⟩\alg{R; 0, -, +, \cdot}⟨R;0,−,+,⋅⟩ consisting of:

  • a set RRR, called the underlying set;
  • a distinguished element 0∈R0 \in R0∈R, called the zero element;
  • a unary operation −:R→R-: R \to R−:R→R, written as x↦−xx \mapsto -xx↦−x, called negation;
  • a binary operation +:R×R→R+: R \times R \to R+:R×R→R, written as (x,y)↦x+y(x, y) \mapsto x + y(x,y)↦x+y, called addition;
  • a binary operation ⋅:R×R→R\cdot: R \times R \to R⋅:R×R→R, written as (x,y)↦x⋅y(x, y) \mapsto x \cdot y(x,y)↦x⋅y, called multiplication;

satisfying the following requirements:

  • Additive structure: ⟨R;0,−,+⟩\alg{R; 0, -, +}⟨R;0,−,+⟩ is an abelian group.
  • Associativity: (x⋅y)⋅z=x⋅(y⋅z)(x \cdot y) \cdot z = x \cdot (y \cdot z)(x⋅y)⋅z=x⋅(y⋅z) for all x,y,z∈Rx, y, z \in Rx,y,z∈R.
  • Left distributivity: x⋅(y+z)=(x⋅y)+(x⋅z)x \cdot (y + z) = (x \cdot y) + (x \cdot z)x⋅(y+z)=(x⋅y)+(x⋅z) for all x,y,z∈Rx, y, z \in Rx,y,z∈R.
  • Right distributivity: (x+y)⋅z=(x⋅z)+(y⋅z)(x + y) \cdot z = (x \cdot z) + (y \cdot z)(x+y)⋅z=(x⋅z)+(y⋅z) for all x,y,z∈Rx, y, z \in Rx,y,z∈R.

Definition: Commutative Rng

A commutative rng is a rng ⟨R;0,−,+,⋅⟩\alg{R; 0, -, +, \cdot}⟨R;0,−,+,⋅⟩ that satisfies the following additional requirement:

  • Commutativity: x⋅y=y⋅xx \cdot y = y \cdot xx⋅y=y⋅x for all x,y∈Rx, y \in Rx,y∈R.

The word “commutative” in the term “commutative rng” emphasizes that the multiplication operation is commutative. By definition, the addition operation is always commutative in every rng.

A field is a commutative ring ⟨K;0,1,−,+,⋅⟩\alg{K; 0, 1, -, +, \cdot}⟨K;0,1,−,+,⋅⟩ that satisfies the following additional requirements:

  • Nontriviality: 0≠10 \ne 10=1.

In abstract algebra, it is common to use the letters FFF and KKK to denote an arbitrary field. The letter KKK comes from “Körper,” the German word for “field.”
