Introduction

Group theory is the first subject in a branch of mathematics known as abstract algebra or modern algebra. Abstract algebra is one of the main branches of modern pure mathematics, and every mathematician, scientist, or engineer who wants a deep understanding of theoretical foundations should know a thing or two about group theory.

The goal of abstract algebra is to study the properties of algebraic systems and the interrelations between them. For example, you probably know about a handful of different number systems, including the integers Z\ZZ, the rational numbers Q\QQ, the real numbers R\RR, and possibly the complex numbers C\CC. You may have learned that there are several algebraic properties that these systems satisfy, such as the commutative property of addition, x+y=y+xx + y = y + xx+y=y+x, and the associative property of multiplication, x⋅(y⋅z)=(x⋅y)⋅zx \cdot (y \cdot z) = (x \cdot y) \cdot zx⋅(y⋅z)=(x⋅y)⋅z.

Definition: Group

A group is an algebraic structure ⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩\alg{G; 1, {}^{-1}, \cdot}⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩ consisting of:

  • a set GGG, called the underlying set;
  • a distinguished element 1∈G1 \in G1∈G, called the identity element;
  • a unary operation −1:G→G{}^{-1}: G \to G−1:G→G, written as x↦x−1x \mapsto x^{-1}x↦x−1, called inversion;
  • a binary operation ⋅:G×G→G\cdot : G \times G \to G⋅:G×G→G, written as (x,y)↦x⋅y(x, y) \mapsto x \cdot y(x,y)↦x⋅y, called the group operation or group product;

satisfying the following requirements:

  • Associative property: (x⋅y)⋅z=x⋅(y⋅z)(x \cdot y) \cdot z = x \cdot (y \cdot z)(x⋅y)⋅z=x⋅(y⋅z) for all x,y,z∈Gx, y, z \in Gx,y,z∈G.
  • Identity property: 1⋅x=x1 \cdot x = x1⋅x=x for all x∈Gx \in Gx∈G.
  • Inverse property: x−1⋅x=1x^{-1} \cdot x = 1x−1⋅x=1 for all x∈Gx \in Gx∈G.

Identity is the Only Idempotent

Theorem: Let ⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩\alg{G; 1, {}^{-1}, \cdot}⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩ be a group. The only element x∈Gx \in Gx∈G having the property that x⋅x=xx \cdot x = xx⋅x=x is the identity element.

Proof: First, to see that the identity element 1∈G1 \in G1∈G actually has the claimed property, observe that 1⋅1=11 \cdot 1 = 11⋅1=1 follows from the identity property.

Next, to see that no other element of GGG has this property, let x∈Gx \in Gx∈G be given, and suppose x⋅x=xx \cdot x = xx⋅x=x. By multiplying both sides by x−1x^{-1}x−1 on the left, we have x−1⋅(x⋅x)=x−1⋅xx^{-1} \cdot (x \cdot x) = x^{-1} \cdot xx−1⋅(x⋅x)=x−1⋅x, from which it follows that:

x=1⋅x=(x−1⋅x)⋅x=x−1⋅(x⋅x)=x−1⋅x=1 x = 1 \cdot x = (x^{-1} \cdot x) \cdot x = x^{-1} \cdot (x \cdot x) = x^{-1} \cdot x = 1 x=1⋅x=(x−1⋅x)⋅x=x−1⋅(x⋅x)=x−1⋅x=1

Thus, we have proven that x⋅x=xx \cdot x = xx⋅x=x implies x=1x = 1x=1. □

Right Identity Property

Theorem: Let ⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩\alg{G; 1, {}^{-1}, \cdot}⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩ be a group. For all x∈Gx \in Gx∈G, we have x⋅1=xx \cdot 1 = xx⋅1=x.

Proof: Let x∈Gx \in Gx∈G be given. We know, from the definition of a group, that x−1⋅x=1x^{-1} \cdot x = 1x−1⋅x=1 and 1⋅x=x1 \cdot x = x1⋅x=x. It follows that:

x⋅1=x⋅(x−1⋅x)=(x⋅x−1)⋅x=1⋅x=x x \cdot 1 = x \cdot (x^{-1} \cdot x) = (x \cdot x^{-1}) \cdot x = 1 \cdot x = x x⋅1=x⋅(x−1⋅x)=(x⋅x−1)⋅x=1⋅x=x

This proves that x⋅1=xx \cdot 1 = xx⋅1=x. □

Inverses are Unique

Theorem: Let ⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩\alg{G; 1, {}^{-1}, \cdot}⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩ be a group. If two elements x,y∈Gx, y \in Gx,y∈G satisfy x⋅y=1x \cdot y = 1x⋅y=1, then x=y−1x = y^{-1}x=y−1 and y=x−1y = x^{-1}y=x−1.

Proof: Let x∈Gx \in Gx∈G be given. We know, from the definition of a group, that x−1⋅x=1x^{-1} \cdot x = 1x−1⋅x=1 and 1⋅x=x1 \cdot x = x1⋅x=x. It follows that:

x⋅1=x⋅(x−1⋅x)=(x⋅x−1)⋅x=1⋅x=x x \cdot 1 = x \cdot (x^{-1} \cdot x) = (x \cdot x^{-1}) \cdot x = 1 \cdot x = x x⋅1=x⋅(x−1⋅x)=(x⋅x−1)⋅x=1⋅x=x

This proves that x⋅1=xx \cdot 1 = xx⋅1=x. □

Definition: Subgroup, H≤GH \le GH≤G

Let ⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩\alg{G; 1, {}^{-1}, \cdot}⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩ be a group. A subgroup of ⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩\alg{G; 1, {}^{-1}, \cdot}⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩ is a subset H⊆GH \subseteq GH⊆G of its underlying set that satisfies the following requirements:

  • 1∈H1 \in H1∈H.
  • If x∈Hx \in Hx∈H, then x−1∈Hx^{-1} \in Hx−1∈H.
  • If x,y∈Hx, y \in Hx,y∈H, then x⋅y∈Hx \cdot y \in Hx⋅y∈H.

We write H≤GH \le GH≤G to denote that HHH is a subgroup of ⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩\alg{G; 1, {}^{-1}, \cdot}⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩.

In other words, HHH is a subgroup of GGG if and only if the structure ⟨H;1,−1∣H,⋅∣H⟩\alg{H; 1, {}^{-1}|_H, \cdot|_H}⟨H;1,−1∣H​,⋅∣H​⟩ is a group in its own right, where −1∣H{}^{-1}|_H−1∣H​ and ⋅∣H\cdot|_H⋅∣H​ denote the inverse operation −1{}^{-1}−1 and the group operation ⋅\cdot⋅ restricted to HHH and H×HH \times HH×H, respectively.

This definition introduces an important notational convention. We will often refer to a group ⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩\alg{G; 1, {}^{-1}, \cdot}⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩ simply by the name of its underlying set GGG, omitting explicit mention of the identity element, the inversion operation, and the group operation.

It is also common in the mathematics literature to not use any symbol, such as x×yx \times yx×y or x⋅yx \cdot yx⋅y, to denote the binary operation in a group. Instead, most mathematicians simply write xyxyxy, using juxtaposition to indicate application of the group operation. Note that the associative property allows us to write expressions like xyzxyzxyz without needing to specify which product should be evaluated first, since both (xy)z(xy)z(xy)z and x(yz)x(yz)x(yz) are guaranteed to produce the same result.

Definition: Abelian Group

An abelian group is a group ⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩\alg{G; 1, {}^{-1}, \cdot}⟨G;1,−1,⋅⟩ that satisfies the following additional requirement:
