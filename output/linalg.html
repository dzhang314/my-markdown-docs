Definition: Vector Space, Vector, Scalar

Let ⟨F;0,1,−F,+F,⋅F⟩\alg{F; 0, 1, -_F, +_F, \cdot_F}⟨F;0,1,−F​,+F​,⋅F​⟩ be a field. A vector space over FFF is an algebraic structure ⟨V;0,−V,+V,⋅V⟩\alg{V; \vo, -_V, +_V, \cdot_V}⟨V;0,−V​,+V​,⋅V​⟩ consisting of:

  • a set VVV, called the underlying set;
  • a distinguished element 0∈V\vo \in V0∈V, called the zero vector;
  • a unary operation −V:V→V-_V: V \to V−V​:V→V, written as v↦−v\vv \mapsto -\vvv↦−v, called vector negation;
  • a binary operation +V:V×V→V+_V: V \times V \to V+V​:V×V→V, written as (v,w)↦v+w(\vv, \vw) \mapsto \vv + \vw(v,w)↦v+w, called vector addition;
  • a binary operation ⋅V:F×V→V\cdot_V: F \times V \to V⋅V​:F×V→V, written as (a,v)↦a⋅v(a, \vv) \mapsto a \cdot \vv(a,v)↦a⋅v, called scalar multiplication;

satisfying the following requirements:

  • Additive structure: ⟨V;0,−V,+V⟩\alg{V; \vo, -_V, +_V}⟨V;0,−V​,+V​⟩ is an abelian group.
  • Identity: 1⋅v=v1 \cdot \vv = \vv1⋅v=v for all v∈V\vv \in Vv∈V.
  • Multiplicative compatibility: (a⋅b)⋅v=a⋅(b⋅v)(a \cdot b) \cdot \vv = a \cdot (b \cdot \vv)(a⋅b)⋅v=a⋅(b⋅v) for all a,b∈Fa, b \in Fa,b∈F and v∈V\vv \in Vv∈V.
  • Left distributivity: a⋅(v+w)=(a⋅v)+(a⋅w)a \cdot (\vv + \vw) = (a \cdot \vv) + (a \cdot \vw)a⋅(v+w)=(a⋅v)+(a⋅w) for all a∈Fa \in Fa∈F and v,w∈V\vv, \vw \in Vv,w∈V.
  • Right distributivity: (a+b)⋅v=(a⋅v)+(b⋅v)(a + b) \cdot \vv = (a \cdot \vv) + (b \cdot \vv)(a+b)⋅v=(a⋅v)+(b⋅v) for all a,b∈Fa, b \in Fa,b∈F and v∈V\vv \in Vv∈V.

The elements of VVV are called vectors, and the elements of FFF are called scalars.

In these notes, we will use boldface upright letters, such as v\vvv and w\vww, to denote vectors. On the other hand, scalars will be denoted by italic letters, such as aaa and bbb. This will allow us to distinguish vector variables from scalar variables at a glance. (This convention is commonly adopted in physics and engineering, but is uncommon in pure mathematics.)

Adopting this convention also provides another benefit: namely, we no longer need to distinguish between the operations of scalar addition +F:F×F→F+_F: F \times F \to F+F​:F×F→F and vector addition +V:V×V→V+_V: V \times V \to V+V​:V×V→V. Instead, we proceed with the implicit understanding that whenever the +++ sign occurs between two scalars, as in a+ba + ba+b, it refers to scalar addition. Likewise, whenever it occurs between two vectors, as in v+w\vv + \vwv+w, it refers to vector addition.

The same goes for the two multiplication operations ⋅F:F×F→F\cdot_F: F \times F \to F⋅F​:F×F→F and ⋅V:F×V→V\cdot_V: F \times V \to V⋅V​:F×V→V. Moving forward, these operations will be denoted by juxtaposition, as in ababab and ava\vvav. The requirement of multiplicative compatibility allows us to write abvab\vvabv without ambiguity, and the identity property allows us to freely insert and remove factors of 111.

As with groups, rings, and fields, it is common to denote a vector space ⟨V;0V,−V,+V,⋅V⟩\alg{V; \vo_V, -_V, +_V, \cdot_V}⟨V;0V​,−V​,+V​,⋅V​⟩ simply by the name of its underlying set VVV. Thus, we will often say “let VVV be a vector space over a field FFF.”

Definition: Linear Combination

Let VVV be a vector space over a field FFF, and let n∈Nn \in \Nn∈N. A linear combination of a finite sequence of vectors v1,v2,…,vn∈V\vv_1, \vv_2, \dots, \vv_n \in Vv1​,v2​,…,vn​∈V is a vector of the form

a1v1+a2v2+⋯+anvn a_1 \vv_1 + a_2 \vv_2 + \cdots + a_n \vv_n a1​v1​+a2​v2​+⋯+an​vn​

where a1,a2,…,an∈Fa_1, a_2, \dots, a_n \in Fa1​,a2​,…,an​∈F. We call the sequence of scalars a1,a2,…,ana_1, a_2, \dots, a_na1​,a2​,…,an​ the coefficients of the linear combination. For n=0n = 0n=0, we define the unique linear combination of the empty sequence of vectors to be 0\vo0.

Definition: Span

Let VVV be a vector space over a field FFF. The span of a subset S⊆VS \subseteq VS⊆V, denoted by span⁡S\vspan SspanS, is the set of all linear combinations of all finite sequences of vectors in SSS.

span⁡S≔{a1v1+a2v2+⋯+anvn:n∈N, a1,a2,…,an∈F, v1,v2,…,vn∈S} \vspan S \coloneqq \{ a_1 \vv_1 + a_2 \vv_2 + \cdots + a_n \vv_n : n \in \N,\ a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n \in F,\ \vv_1, \vv_2, \dots, \vv_n \in S \} spanS:={a1​v1​+a2​v2​+⋯+an​vn​:n∈N, a1​,a2​,…,an​∈F, v1​,v2​,…,vn​∈S}

Because we defined 0\vo0 to be the unique linear combination of an empty set of vectors, we always have 0∈span⁡S\vo \in \vspan S0∈spanS for any subset S⊆VS \subseteq VS⊆V, including the empty set.

Definition: Linear Subspace

Let VVV be a vector space over a field FFF. A linear subspace of VVV is a subset S⊆VS \subseteq VS⊆V that is closed under taking linear combinations, i.e., every linear combination of every finite sequence of vectors in SSS is itself in SSS.

Note that the empty set ∅⊆V\varnothing \subseteq V∅⊆V is not a linear subspace! The requirement of closure under linear combinations includes the empty linear combination, which the empty set fails to satisfy, since 0∉∅\vo \notin \varnothing0∈/∅. Because of this requirement, a linear subspace must always contain the zero vector.

Pairwise Linear Combinations Suffice

Theorem: Let VVV be a vector space over a field FFF. A subset S⊆VS \subseteq VS⊆V is a linear subspace of VVV if and only if SSS has the following two properties:

  • Contains the zero vector: 0∈S\vo \in S0∈S.
  • Closed under pairwise linear combinations: For all a,b∈Fa, b \in Fa,b∈F and v,w∈S\vv, \vw \in Sv,w∈S, we have av+bw∈Sa\vv + b\vw \in Sav+bw∈S.

Proof sketch: We prove by induction on n∈Nn \in \Nn∈N that SSS is closed under taking nnn-ary linear combinations. The first property handles the base case n=0n = 0n=0, and the second property handles the inductive step, using the fact that an nnn-ary linear combination can be written as a binary linear combination of a vector and an (n−1)(n-1)(n−1)-ary linear combination. ∎

Definition: Affine Combination

Let VVV be a vector space over a field FFF, and let n∈Nn \in \Nn∈N. An affine combination of a finite sequence of vectors v1,v2,…,vn∈V\vv_1, \vv_2, \dots, \vv_n \in Vv1​,v2​,…,vn​∈V is a vector of the form a1v1+a2v2+⋯+anvna_1 \vv_1 + a_2 \vv_2 + \cdots + a_n \vv_na1​v1​+a2​v2​+⋯+an​vn​ where the coefficients a1,a2,…,an∈Fa_1, a_2, \dots, a_n \in Fa1​,a2​,…,an​∈F satisfy a1+a2+⋯+an=1a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_n = 1a1​+a2​+⋯+an​=1.

Note that, unlike linear combinations, there is no such thing as an empty affine combination. The sum of an empty sequence of scalars is 000, and by the definition of a field, 0≠10 \ne 10=1.

Definition: Affine Span, Affine Hull

Let VVV be a vector space over a field FFF. The affine span or affine hull of a subset S⊆VS \subseteq VS⊆V, denoted by aff⁡S\aff SaffS, is the set of all affine combinations of all finite sequences of vectors in SSS.

Definition: Affine Set

Let VVV be a vector space over a field FFF. An affine set in VVV is a subset S⊆VS \subseteq VS⊆V that is closed under taking affine combinations, i.e., every affine combination of every finite sequence of vectors in SSS is itself in SSS.

Because there are no empty affine combinations, the empty set ∅∈V\varnothing \in V∅∈V does (vacuously) qualify as an affine set.

Definition: Conic Hull

Let VVV be a vector space over an ordered field (F,<)(F, <)(F,<). The conic hull of a set S⊆VS \subseteq VS⊆V, denoted by cone⁡S\cone SconeS, is the set of all conic combinations of all finite sequences of vectors in SSS.

Definition: Cone

Let VVV be a vector space over an ordered field (F,<)(F, <)(F,<). A cone in VVV is a subset S⊆VS \subseteq VS⊆V that is closed under scalar multiplication by nonnegative scalars, i.e., if a∈Fa \in Fa∈F satisfies a≥0a \ge 0a≥0 and v∈S\vv \in Sv∈S, then av∈Sa\vv \in Sav∈S.

Note that a cone is not necessarily closed under taking conic combinations!

Let VVV be a vector space over an ordered field (F,<)(F, <)(F,<), and let n∈Nn \in \Nn∈N. A convex combination of a finite sequence of vectors v1,v2,…,vn∈V\vv_1, \vv_2, \dots, \vv_n \in Vv1​,v2​,…,vn​∈V is a vector of the form a1v1+a2v2+⋯+anvna_1 \vv_1 + a_2 \vv_2 + \cdots + a_n \vv_na1​v1​+a2​v2​+⋯+an​vn​ where the coefficients a1,a2,…,an∈Fa_1, a_2, \dots, a_n \in Fa1​,a2​,…,an​∈F satisfy a1+a2+⋯+an=1a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_n = 1a1​+a2​+⋯+an​=1 and are all nonnegative, i.e., ai≥0a_i \ge 0ai​≥0 for all i∈{1,2,…,n}i \in \{1, 2, \dots, n \}i∈{1,2,…,n}.

Definition: Convex Hull

Let VVV be a vector space over an ordered field (F,<)(F, <)(F,<). The convex hull of a set S⊆VS \subseteq VS⊆V, denoted by conv⁡S\conv SconvS, is the set of all convex combinations of all finite sequences of vectors in SSS.

Definition: Convex Set

Let VVV be a vector space over an ordered field (F,<)(F, <)(F,<). A convex set in VVV is a subset S⊆VS \subseteq VS⊆V that is closed under taking convex combinations, i.e., every convex combination of every finite sequence of vectors in SSS is itself in SSS.

Definition: Real Vector Space, Complex Vector Space

A real vector space is a vector space over the field R\RR of real numbers. Similarly, a complex vector space is a vector space over the field C\CC of complex numbers.
